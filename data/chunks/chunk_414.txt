According to the Ministry of Agriculture and Farmers Welfare, the total production of millets in India during 2020-21 was estimated to be around 28.7 million tonnes. The total production of minor millets in India during 2019-2020 was around 3.9 million tonnes, with Tamil Nadu being the largest producer (1.3 million tonnes) followed by Karnataka (1.0 million tonnes) and Andhra Pradesh (0.6 million tonnes). The production of minor millets in other states such as Maharashtra, Gujarat, and Telangana was also substantial. In recent years, there has been renewed interest in millets due to their health benefits and the potential they hold for improving food security and sustainable agriculture. The Indian government has also launched various schemes and initiatives to promote millet cultivation and consumption, including the Millets Mission under the National Food Security Mission. Storage scenario of minor millets In India, there are several government agencies responsible for regulating and monitoring the storage and distribution of millets. Major ones are Food Corporation of India (FCI), National Agricultural Cooperative Marketing Federation of India (NAFED), State Agriculture Marketing Boards (SAMB), Agricultural Produce Market Committees (APMCs), and Warehousing Development and Regulatory Authority (WDRA). The approximate share of storage out of production of minor millets in India varies depending on several factors such as the crop, location, and storage capacity. Generally, it is estimated that 5-15% of the total production of minor millets in India is stored, with majority of the grain being consumed or sold immediately after harvest. FCI is responsible for maintaining adequate storage facilities for various food grains including minor millets, and as of 2019, the FCI had a total storage capacity of around 76.5 million tonnes. However, the actual utilization of this capacity varies, and it is estimated that the utilization rate for minor millets is much lower than for other major crops like rice and wheat. Additionally, farmers often store small quantities of minor millets in their homes or in local community storage facilities for personal consumption or to sell in the local market. The exact amount of storage at this level is difficult to estimate, but it is a common practice in rural areas.
